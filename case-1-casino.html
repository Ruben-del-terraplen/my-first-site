§ 01 — Context

A landing page doing too much, converting too little.

The problem

The casino landing page was the highest-traffic surface in the loyalty experience — and the lowest performing. Guests arrived looking to play, see promotions, or check tournament schedules, but were greeted with a fragmented page that buried every action under marketing copy.

Conversion to "find a slot" and "view tournament" had declined for four consecutive quarters. The page hadn't been touched since 2019, and analytics showed 62% of guests bouncing within ten seconds.

§ 03 — Process

Six weeks of listening, eight weeks of building.

P. 01
Onboard research
Spent two weeks aboard ship interviewing 18 guests at varying stages of their cruise — from disembarkation-day veterans to first-night newcomers. Mapped the actual journey from cabin → casino → action.
P. 02
Job-to-be-done framing
Synthesized findings into four primary intents: see what's happening tonight, find a specific game, redeem points, ask for help. Restructured the IA around these — not around marketing categories.
P. 03
Component-first design
Built every section as a component in our design system so promotions teams could ship updates without engineering. The page became a composable layout, not a fixed artwork.
P. 04
Live A/B with cohort split
Rolled out to two ships first as a true A/B, with the legacy page on identical traffic. Measured engagement, conversion, and a qualitative satisfaction score across two sailings before fleet-wide release.

§ 06 — Reflection

What I'd do differently.

Looking back

The biggest unlock was getting onboard early — every assumption we held in the office about "what guests want" survived contact with reality only partially. I'd budget more time for that next time.

I'd also push harder on the analytics instrumentation upfront. We retrofitted events after launch, which made the first round of post-ship measurement noisier than it needed to be.
