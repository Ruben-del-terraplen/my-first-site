§ 01 — Context

A 14-deck ship
flattened into a list.

The problem

Selecting a stateroom is the highest-consideration moment in the cruise booking funnel — guests spend an average of 17 minutes on this single step. Yet our flow showed staterooms as a flat, paginated list with thumbnail icons and no spatial context.

Customer service tickets analyzed across 18 months showed 34% of post-booking complaints traced back to "not what I expected" room placement — too far from elevators, near nightlife, obstructed views.

"Guests aren't picking a room. They're picking a view, a vibe, a walk to dinner." — Research synthesis, sailing study

§ 03 — Process

A research-led 21 weeks.

P. 01
Diary studies, two sailings
Recruited 12 first-time and 8 repeat cruisers across two sailings. Asked them to journal the moment they realized something about their cabin — good or bad. Surfaced 47 distinct "expectation gaps."
P. 02
Spatial prototyping
Worked directly with the ship-engineering team to license deck plans as data. Prototyped three navigation models — list, deck-by-deck, and 3D — and usability tested with 24 guests.
P. 03
Photography commissioning
Partnered with marketing to commission cabin-by-cabin view photography across the fleet — over 4,000 unique cabins. The biggest content effort of the project, but the single highest-impact addition.
P. 04
Cross-platform parity
Designed the system once with platform-aware adaptations — the deck navigator works on web with hover, on iOS with peek, on Android with long-press. Engineering shipped all three from one design source.

§ 05 — Reflection

What I'd do again.

Looking back

The decision to commission real photography — instead of using illustrations or 3D renders — was the right call but a hard sell. It required cross-team buy-in I hadn't budgeted relationship time for.

If I were starting again I'd involve customer service in design reviews from week one. Their data shaped this project more than any other source, and including them earlier would have saved iteration cycles.
