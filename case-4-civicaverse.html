§ 01 — Problem

No site. Misaligned inquiries.

The problem

The company lacked a digital presence that clearly communicated its structural engineering services and limitations. Without a website, potential clients often reached out with requests outside the firm's scope, creating unnecessary back-and-forth and operational friction.

The challenge was to rapidly design and implement a lean, informative website that filters inquiries by setting clear expectations upfront.

§ 02 — Context

Two weeks. Clarity as the deliverable.

The context

This was a fast-paced Product Design engagement focused on delivering a foundational web experience for a small structural engineering firm. With limited time, the goal was to prioritize clarity, speed, and essential content.

The site needed to convey the company's identity, core offerings, and boundaries in a way that minimized misaligned inquiries. The final product included a Landing Page, Services Overview, and About Us section — each crafted to support intuitive scanning, trust-building, and efficient decision-making for prospective clients.

The brief wasn't to build a website. It was to filter inquiries — by stating clearly what the firm did, and what it didn't.
§ 03 — Process

Designed and shipped
in fourteen days.

P. 01
Discovery
Conversations with the business owner to understand the firm's actual scope versus what clients commonly requested. Identified the gap that was creating misaligned inquiries — and made closing it the design objective.
P. 02
Information architecture
Distilled the site to three pages — Landing, Services, About — with a deliberate focus on scope clarity. Every section answered "is this firm right for my project?" before "how do I contact them?"
P. 03
Visual identity & design
Built a small-but-coherent design system: typography, palette, and component style. Designed the three pages with intuitive scanning patterns and trust-building visual cues for prospective clients.
P. 04
Handoff
Delivered to implementation with clear specs and assets. The final product gave the firm a foundation it could grow from, not a polished façade it had to maintain.

What this delivered

Delivered the firm's first owned digital presence in two weeks. The site established a clear scope boundary upfront — letting prospective clients self-qualify before reaching out — and replaced the inquiry friction with a coherent first impression.

Beyond the immediate launch, the small design system created during this sprint gave the firm a foundation to grow from rather than a one-off page to maintain.

§ 06 — Lessons & next steps

What I'd carry forward.

Looking back

The constraint — two weeks — was the design tool. It forced ruthless prioritization: every page had one job, every section had one purpose. Larger projects rarely give you that clarity.

Reframing the brief from "build a website" to "filter inquiries" was the unlock. I'd start every small-business engagement that way: what's the cost of the status quo, and what's the smallest design that addresses it?
