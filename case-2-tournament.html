§ 01 — Context

Empty seats at $25,000 prize tournaments.

The problem

Slot tournaments are one of the most lucrative onboard activations — but week-over-week attendance had been declining. Guests didn't know they were happening, didn't understand entry, and the existing landing page read like a legal document.

The signup form sat six taps deep from the cabin tablet home, behind a login wall, and asked for information the guest had already provided at boarding.

"By the time a guest finds the tournament, the tournament has already started." — Casino Host, Royal Princess

§ 03 — Process

Three constraints
that shaped the design.

P. 01
Pre-fill from boarding
Worked with the data team to surface the guest's loyalty number, room, and signed waivers automatically — eliminating the "I already told you this" friction. Entry dropped from six fields to one tap.
P. 02
Live social proof
Surfaced live entry counts and prize pool in real-time as social proof. Designed a quiet animated meter that didn't feel gimmicky but communicated "this is filling up."
P. 03
Promotion in-context
Surfaced upcoming tournaments contextually on the casino landing and in-cabin home — pulling discovery upstream so guests learned about events before they were stranded looking.

§ 05 — Reflection

What it taught me.

Looking back

This project reinforced something I keep relearning: the best UX win is often removing the form, not redesigning it. The first design draft tried to make the multi-step flow feel friendlier — the shipped one removed five of the steps entirely.

It also shifted how I think about marketing surfaces. A "landing page" isn't a destination — it's a hand-off. Designing it as a hand-off changed everything about the IA.
